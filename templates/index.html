What is an AHP Hierarchy?

The Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP) structures a complex decision problem as a six-level tree. The Goal sits at the top. Below it, Actors represent the stakeholders whose preferences shape the decision. The chosen Investment Horizon determines the planning window, and four possible Macro Scenarios capture market uncertainty. Criteria (and their sub-criteria) define the dimensions of value, while the seven Asset Classes are the decision alternatives. Each pairwise comparison propagates upward to synthesise a final weighted allocation.

How Sub-Criteria Feed Into the Final Allocation
Step 1
Evidence
Live market data is fetched: β, σ, Max Drawdown, E[R]%, Dividend Yield for each of 7 assets.
Step 2
Sub-Criteria Matrix
AI compares each asset pair on every sub-criterion (e.g. "Is SPY safer than IWM on Volatility?"). This fills 7×7 matrices.
Step 3
Criteria Score
Sub-criteria scores are synthesised into a single Return score and Risk score per asset using the geometric mean method.
Step 4
Criteria Matrix
The 4 criteria (Return, Risk, Liquidity, Diversification) are compared pairwise. Return typically weights ~40%, Risk ~35%.
Step 5
Final Allocation
AHP synthesises all levels into portfolio weights, applies policy constraints, and runs 1,000 Monte Carlo checks.

Risk Sub-Criteria Weights
Within the Risk criterion, how do you weight Beta vs Volatility vs Max Drawdown vs Liquidity Risk? These comparisons affect how each risk dimension scores each asset. (Informational — shows the model's calibrated values.)
Beta vs Volatility AI: Equal (1.0) Both capture market sensitivity. β is systemic risk; σ captures total price uncertainty.
Beta vs Max Drawdown AI: Slight Max DD (0.5) Max Drawdown directly measures the worst experienced loss — more intuitive for trustees.
Beta vs Liquidity Risk AI: Moderate Beta (3.0) Liquidity risk matters more in crisis; beta matters in normal markets.
Volatility vs Max Drawdown AI: Equal (1.0) Both reflect downside exposure; σ is forward-looking, Max DD is historical worst case.
Volatility vs Liquidity Risk AI: Moderate Vol (2.0) Volatility affects the fund daily; liquidity risk surfaces only in crisis.
Max Drawdown vs Liquidity Risk AI: Moderate Max DD (2.0) Max DD is the funded ratio threat; liquidity risk is a cash flow management issue.
Return Sub-Criteria Weights
Within the Return criterion, how do you weight Expected Return vs Dividend Yield vs Growth Potential? Income-focused funds prefer dividend yield; growth-focused funds prefer capital appreciation. (Informational — shows the model's calibrated values.)
Expected Return vs Dividend Yield AI: Strong E[R] (5.0) Total return dominates for long-horizon funds; dividend yield matters more for near-payout funds.
Expected Return vs Growth Potential AI: Moderate E[R] (3.0) Growth potential is longer-horizon; expected return captures near-term earnings power.
Dividend Yield vs Growth Potential AI: Slight Growth (0.5) For a 30-year pension fund, growth potential compounds more powerfully than income.
